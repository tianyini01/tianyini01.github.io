PROSODIC PHONOLOGY · SOUND CHANGE · MODELING

Tianyi Ni

I am a Ph.D. student in Linguistics at The Ohio State University. I study how phonetic cues such as duration and F0 become organized into prosodic grammar, including tone, quantity, tonal accent, moraic structure, and metrical contrasts.

My work combines formal phonological analysis with acoustic and corpus evidence, production and perception experiments, artificial-language learning, and computational models of sound change. I use these tools to investigate how phonetic cue relations become phonological structure, and what this process reveals about prosodic representation, contrast, and phonological typology.

Research

Research focus

Prosodic cue reorganization

How do duration and F0 become reorganized into tone, quantity, tonal accent, and metrical structure? This line of work asks how phonetic cue relations become prosodic grammar through sound change.

Contrast stability and sound change

Why do some phonological contrasts persist, merge, or reorganize? I use production–perception experiments and computational models to study contrast maintenance, near-merger, cue reliability, and functional load.

Tone and prosody in language systems

I study tone, laryngeal cues, and prosodic structure in Germanic and Southeast Asian languages, with additional work on quantitative morphology and lexical structure.

Selected projects

Featured work

F0–duration cue reorganization in prosodic sound change

Current dissertation direction. I am developing a production–perception model of how duration and F0 are reorganized into prosodic contrasts, and why some resulting systems are possible but typologically rare.

Ternary vowel quantity in East Frisian Low German

With Björn Köhnlein. This project analyzes a rare three-way vowel quantity contrast using metrical structure and asks how prosodic systems can stabilize unusually dense duration contrasts.

Cantonese tone near-merger

With Rebecca L. Morley. This project models how production, perception, functional load, and lexical structure shape the persistence or collapse of a tone contrast.

Khmer word formation
