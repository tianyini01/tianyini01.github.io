Research

Research

I study how sound systems maintain, lose, and reorganize contrasts. My work is grounded in phonological theory, but I use evidence from phonetics, corpora, experiments, artificial-language learning, and computational modeling to ask how phonological systems arise and change over time.

My current research focuses on prosodic structure and sound change. I am especially interested in how phonetic cues such as duration and F0 become organized into phonological systems such as tone, quantity, tonal accent, moraic structure, and foot-based contrasts. A central question in my work is why some prosodic systems are possible but typologically rare: are these systems excluded by universal grammatical constraints, or do they arise only under narrow production-perception-learning pathways?

Overview

Research questions, not just methods.

I organize my work around empirical programs: how phonetic cues become prosodic grammar, why contrasts persist or merge, how tone and laryngeal cues interact in Southeast Asian languages, and how lexical structure can be studied quantitatively. Methods such as acoustic analysis, statistical modeling, corpus work, and simulations are tools for answering these phonological questions.

01

Prosodic Structure, Cue Reorganization, and Sound Change

This line of work investigates how duration, F0, and prosodic structure interact in the emergence of tone, quantity, and metrical contrasts. I am particularly interested in cases where prosodic systems appear to reverse expected cue relations or maintain unusually dense contrasts, such as ternary vowel quantity, tonal-accent systems, and duration-F0 reversals.

A central idea is that phonetic cues do not simply replace one another during sound change. Instead, cues can be reorganized: duration may first condition the emergence of pitch movement, while later tonal categories may develop their own durational implementation. These cases provide a way to ask how phonetic cue relations become prosodic grammar.

02

Production-Perception Dynamics and Contrast Stability

This line of work asks why some phonological contrasts persist, merge, or reorganize over time. I use computational models and experimental evidence to study how production biases, perceptual ambiguity, lexical structure, and functional load shape the stability of contrasts.

My current work in this area focuses on Cantonese tone near-merger. The broader question is how production and perception can become misaligned, and how such misalignment affects the long-term dynamics of contrast maintenance, merger, and re-stabilization.

Related work

  • Learning near-merger: cue reliability and bidirectional regularization with Yue Yin and Rebecca L. Morley; presented at AMP 2026.
  • A cognitive model for production-perception misalignment with Rebecca L. Morley; manuscript in preparation.

03

Tone, Laryngeal Cues, and Southeast Asian Phonology

A third strand of my work investigates the interaction of tone, voicing, F0, and prosodic domains in Southeast Asian languages. These projects ask how laryngeal and tonal cues are represented, how they interact with prosodic structure, and how they can be reanalyzed through sound change.

Related work

  • Bidirectional consonantal effects on F0 realization in Burmese presented at the Third International Conference on Tone and Intonation.
  • A domain-driven phonological analysis of sandhi-voicing in Modern Burmese presented at the Southeast Asian Linguistics Society.
  • Burmese sandhi-voicing: From the perspective of emergent phonology published in Coyote Papers. Paper Abstract

04

Quantitative Morphology and Lexical Structure

In addition to my work in phonology, I also study lexical structure and word formation using corpus and quantitative methods. This work asks how morphological systems organize semantic relationships, productivity, and lexical family structure.

My current project in this area investigates Khmer word formation, comparing historical prefixation with modern compounding. The project asks whether so-called fossilized prefixes are semantically random or whether they retain measurable structure, and how this compares to productive compounding.

Related work

  • Fossilized Prefixes, Living Compounds? Productivity and Semantic Shift in Khmer Word Formation presented at the 35th Annual Meeting of the Southeast Asian Linguistics Society. Slides Abstract
  • Semantic transparency and productivity of modern Khmer compounds and derivational prefixation with Andrea Sims; manuscript in preparation.
  • The Phonology and Morphology of Siriano: A Grammar Sketch M.A. thesis, University of Arizona. Thesis

Methods

Methods

Across these projects, I combine formal phonological analysis with quantitative and experimental methods. My work uses acoustic analysis, corpus methods, statistical modeling, artificial-language learning, and computational simulations of sound change. I treat these methods as tools for answering phonological questions: how contrasts are represented, how they change, and why some systems are common while others are possible but rare.
